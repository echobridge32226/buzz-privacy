About the Developer

Who built Buzz, and why

A personal account of the problem that sparked this app, and why I couldn't stop working on it.

S
Sunny Shah
Founder, Buzz Captions & Translation
Who I am

A little about me

Hi, I'm Sunny. Born and raised in Mumbai, India - and yes, it's as chaotic and wonderful as it sounds. I went to the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign for applied math and biochemistry, then Stanford for a master's in computational and mathematical engineering. These days I work as a Senior Machine Learning Engineer at Oak Street Health (part of CVS Health), and I'm based in Chicago.

Outside of work: I have a dog named Selene who absolutely runs my life. I read a lot of science fiction, I'm always somewhere in the middle of learning a new language (6 and counting!), and I am unapologetically obsessed with reality TV - RuPaul's Drag Race and Real Housewives of Atlanta are non-negotiable.

One more thing, and it's kind of the whole point: I was born deaf. I got my first hearing aids at 16 months old, and cochlear implants at age 7 and again at 11. Growing up Deaf in a hearing world shapes how you move through it - and it's the reason Buzz exists.

The problem

The moment I realized something was broken

In March 2026, I was dealing with listening fatigue. After a full day of work, I'd take my cochlear implants off - and my partner still wanted to talk.

I tried the iOS live captioning feature. It worked, kind of. But it always made things awkward: either I'd wait a few seconds to read the captions before responding (which killed the flow), or I'd crane my neck to catch up faster (which isn't exactly a natural way to have a conversation). None of the other transcription apps had a simple rotate feature - something that would let two people face each other and actually go back and forth.

That was one side of it. The other came from growing up in India, where knowing only English was its own kind of isolation. Some "friends" would deliberately switch languages mid-conversation to shut me out. I heard the same story again and again from members of HearBuds - a community I started back in 2020 - Deaf people finding creative, exhausting ways to stay included in a world that wasn't built for them.

So I thought: what if one app did both? Real-time transcription and translation, built for people who actually need it. That's how Buzz was born.

"Some friends would deliberately speak in a language I didn't know to isolate me from the conversation. I heard that story too many times."
The impact

When it started to matter

My partner and I were on holiday in Mexico when we wandered into a mezcaleria for a tasting. The guide - explaining the flavors, the process, the stories behind each bottle - only spoke Spanish. I don't speak Spanish.

Before Buzz, that would've meant nodding along, pretending to follow, missing the whole thing. Instead, I opened the app, pointed the mic, and it captioned and translated in real time. Spanish in, English out. I was part of the conversation, not hovering outside it. By the end of the night I was swapping stories with the guide and chatting with the other travelers.

It was the first time I remember thinking: yeah, this actually works.

"Never will I ever be left out of a conversation, if I can help it."
What I've learned

What building Buzz taught me

I am not a mobile developer. I went into this knowing machine learning and math, and not much else. Building Buzz meant doing a lot of research, leaning on AI tools to fill the gaps, and stepping in every time those tools went sideways - which happened more than I'd like to admit. Setting up the infrastructure, figuring out what capabilities even existed, learning to actually ship something real - all of it was new territory.

The technical side was hard. But the bigger lesson was simpler: you have to listen. The beta testers who gave me honest feedback made this app what it is. Their perspective - what didn't work, what was missing, what felt wrong - shaped every meaningful improvement and made Buzz more usable for Deaf folks navigating the hearing world.

I'm not done learning. But I'm genuinely excited to hear how people are using Buzz and what it's made possible for them.

What's next

Where Buzz is going

I want Buzz to be the app that removes communication barriers - everywhere, for everyone.

One thing I'd love to add: speech-to-sign-language support. Not every Deaf person is comfortable relying on written captions alone. Sign language is a full language, and if Buzz can bridge that gap too, it should.

What still surprises me is that this app didn't already exist. The world is more connected than ever, AI has made transcription and translation genuinely good - but nobody had put them together for the people who need both at once. Every app I found either did transcription or translation. And the translation apps were built for language learners and travelers, not Deaf people trying to navigate everyday life.

There's a lot more to build. I'm looking forward to it.
